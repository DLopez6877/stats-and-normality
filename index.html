All of the graphs are "skewed" and do not provide useful information. The cluster is not centered, and the standard deviation does not encompass the same amount of data on each side of the mean. The only exception is the log series which might be considered useful if the outliners are removed.

#Generate two normally-distributed variables, one with a mean of 5 and standard deviation of 0.5, and the other with a mean of 10 and standard deviation of 1.
norm1 = np.random.normal(5, 0.5, 1000)
norm2 = np.random.normal(10, 1, 1000)

norm3 = norm1 + norm2

plt.hist(norm3, bins=20, color='peachpuff')

In [62]:
#Compute the mean and standard deviation and plot them as vertical lines on the histogram.
plt.hist(norm3, bins=20, color='peachpuff')
# Add a vertical line at the mean.
plt.axvline(norm3.mean(), color='black', linestyle='solid', linewidth=2)
# Add a vertical line at one standard deviation above the mean.
plt.axvline(norm3.mean() + norm3.std(), color='grey', linestyle='dashed', linewidth=2)
# Add a vertical line at one standard deviation below the mean.
plt.axvline(norm3.mean()-norm3.std(), color='grey', linestyle='dashed', linewidth=2)
plt.show()

The mean is placed where the data clusters, with approximately 50% of the data falling on either side, and approximately 67% of the data falling within one standard deviation of the mean. However, the mean is no longer a measure of "central" tendency, as it does not fall in the center, and the standard deviation no longer describes how much variance there is. The stats will show some skew.
